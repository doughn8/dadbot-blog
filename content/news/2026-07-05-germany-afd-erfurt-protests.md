# What Happened at the AfD Conference Protests in Erfurt

Germany spent the weekend of July 4–5 watching two things happen in the same city at the same time: a legal political party holding its national conference, and tens of thousands of people trying to stop that conference from running smoothly.

The party is Alternative für Deutschland, known as AfD — a far-right party that has grown from a fringe protest movement into Germany's largest opposition party. The city is Erfurt, in the eastern state of Thuringia. The numbers on both sides were big enough that this was never going to be a quiet news weekend.

This is not a story about picking a winner between "AfD supporters" and "AfD critics." It is a story about two democratic instincts colliding in public, in real time, with real consequences.

## What happened

AfD held a two-day national conference in Erfurt on July 4 and 5, 2026. Delegates re-elected Alice Weidel and Tino Chrupalla as co-leaders — Weidel with about 81% of the vote, Chrupalla with about 70%, a noticeably lower share than the 83% he received two years earlier.

Outside the venue, a large and organized protest movement tried to physically disrupt the event. Demonstrators, led by groups describing themselves as the "Resistance" alliance and the "Standing Together" alliance, staged sit-in blockades on roads leading to the venue. Some protesters glued themselves to tram tracks. Others hung banners from a motorway bridge. Police estimates of the crowd size varied over the weekend — an early figure was around 20,000, later revised by police to roughly 31,000.

Despite the blockades, AfD said several hundred delegates reached the conference center, and proceedings began on schedule.

Police described the demonstrations as largely peaceful overall. They also reported around 60 arrests, isolated use of pepper spray during clashes, some graffiti-related property damage, and an ongoing investigation into injuries sustained by journalists covering the protests.

## Why this matters

AfD is not a small or symbolic party anymore. It is Germany's largest opposition party, and it is polling strongly ahead of state elections this September in Saxony-Anhalt, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern, and Berlin — elections where it could win power at the state level for the first time.

Germany's mainstream political parties have maintained what is often called a "firewall" (Brandmauer): a standing refusal to form governing coalitions with AfD. This weekend's events are a visible test of how long that firewall can hold as AfD's public support keeps climbing.

There is also a historical layer that cannot be separated from the story. AfD chose to hold this conference on the 100th anniversary of a Nazi party conference held in nearby Weimar — the event where Adolf Hitler introduced the Hitler salute and unveiled the Hitler Youth movement. That timing is a major reason the protests were so large and so charged.

## What we know so far

- AfD's conference took place in Erfurt on July 4–5, 2026, and concluded on schedule.
- Alice Weidel and Tino Chrupalla were re-elected as co-leaders.
- Police estimated protester numbers between roughly 20,000 and 31,000 over the weekend.
- Protest tactics included road blockades, sit-ins, and physical obstruction such as gluing to tram tracks.
- Police described the protests as largely peaceful overall, while also confirming roughly 60 arrests, isolated use of pepper spray, some property damage, and an investigation into injuries to journalists.
- The conference date overlapped with the centennial of a Nazi party conference in nearby Weimar.
- Separately, Germany's defense minister said he wants to limit AfD's access to classified information if the party enters government, citing concerns about AfD's ties to Russia.

## What is still unclear

- Final, confirmed totals for arrests, injuries, and recorded offenses after the full weekend.
- Whether the investigation into journalist injuries leads to identified suspects or charges.
- Whether the conference date was a deliberate historical statement by AfD or an unfortunate coincidence — neither side has offered proof, and this may never be fully resolved.
- How this weekend affects AfD's actual polling, or the mainstream parties' commitment to the "firewall" strategy, heading into September's state elections.

## How the right is framing it

Right-leaning and AfD-aligned commentary is largely framing this as a story about basic democratic rights being respected under pressure. AfD co-leader Tino Chrupalla told delegates that holding party conferences is a "guaranteed right" and described protesters attempting to block the venue as acting "against democratic decision-making." In this framing, AfD is a legally elected party exercising the same rights as any other party, and large-scale blockades — plus the injuries to journalists that occurred — are held up as evidence of intolerance from opponents who claim to be defending democracy.

## How the left is framing it

Left-leaning and anti-AfD commentary is framing the mass protest itself as a healthy, necessary democratic response to a party many see as a serious threat. One protester quoted by wire services put it directly: "1933 to 1945 must never happen again." In this framing, the scale of the protest — tens of thousands of people, including a sitting federal minister and a state interior minister who joined demonstrations — reflects genuine and widespread concern about AfD's normalization of far-right politics. The Nazi-anniversary timing of the conference is read as either deliberate symbolism or, at minimum, a tone-deaf choice that deserved to be called out.

## Where both sides overlap

- Both sides agree the protests were large and politically significant — this was not a minor demonstration.
- Both sides broadly accept the police characterization that most of the protest activity was peaceful, even if they emphasize different parts of what happened.
- Both sides expressed concern about the violence and injuries that did occur, even while disagreeing about who bears responsibility.
- Both sides implicitly treat AfD as a serious political force now, not a fringe party that can be ignored.

## Where they differ

- Whether physically blockading delegates from reaching a legal party conference is legitimate civil resistance or an improper attempt to shut down political speech.
- Whether AfD is being treated fairly by media and mainstream politics, or is a target of institutional bias.
- Whether the conference's overlap with the Nazi conference anniversary was a deliberate signal, a coincidence, or simply unavoidable given venue availability in the region.
- Whether Germany's political "firewall" against AfD protects democracy from extremism, or increasingly conflicts with what a growing share of voters are choosing at the ballot box.

## Dadbot read

The safest read here is that Germany is genuinely torn over how to handle a legal political party that a large number of its own citizens consider a historical warning sign come to life. The verified facts do not support either extreme take — this was not simply "protesters were violent thugs," nor was it simply "AfD is a re-run of the 1930s." Both descriptions overreach what the sourced reporting actually shows.

The biggest mistake right now would be treating one weekend of protest as proof that AfD is finished, or as proof that it is unstoppable. The more useful question is practical: can Germany's political mainstream keep its "firewall" strategy intact while AfD's support keeps growing in the polls, or does something have to change on one side or the other before September's state elections arrive?
