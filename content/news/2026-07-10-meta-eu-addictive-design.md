# Why the EU Says Facebook and Instagram Are Designed to Keep Us Scrolling

You open Instagram to check one message. Ten minutes later, you are watching a video from somebody you do not follow, about something you were not looking for, with no clear memory of deciding to stay.

That missing stopping point is now at the centre of a European Union case against Meta.

On 10 July 2026, the European Commission said its preliminary view is that Facebook and Instagram may encourage compulsive use. It named features including infinite scrolling, autoplaying videos and personalised recommendations, with particular concern about children and teenagers.

Meta disagrees. It says the findings fail to account for protections it has introduced through Teen Accounts and parental controls.

First, the procedural bit that several headlines blur: **this is not a final ruling, and Meta has not been fined in this case.**

## What the EU has—and has not—decided

The European Commission has issued preliminary findings under the Digital Services Act, the EU law that places risk-management duties on very large online platforms.

The Commission's current position is that Meta has not done enough to assess and reduce risks created by the design of Facebook and Instagram. It is especially concerned about younger people using the platforms excessively or late at night.

Meta can inspect the evidence and submit a formal response. The Commission must consider that response before reaching a final decision.

For now, the Commission has set out its case and Meta has disputed it. No final breach, fine or required redesign has been established. Nor is this a medical finding that every Facebook or Instagram user is addicted.

## What does “addictive design” mean?

“Addictive design” is the Commission's term for features that may encourage people to keep using a service compulsively.

The concern is not that one swipe is medically addictive. It is that several design choices remove pauses and keep offering another reason to stay.

A newspaper has a final page. A television programme ends. Even a DVD menu eventually sits still and judges you. An infinite feed has no natural finish line.

## The features under scrutiny

Most users will recognise the features named in the case:

- **Infinite scroll** removes the moment when you reach the bottom and decide whether to continue.
- **Autoplay** starts another video before you make a fresh choice.
- **Personalised recommendations** continually select content predicted to hold your attention.
- **Reels and Stories** make moving from one short item to the next almost effortless.
- **Dismissible reminders** may tell you how much time has passed while making it easy to carry on.

Autoplay can be convenient. Recommendations can uncover genuinely useful material. A teenager watching three Reels is not evidence of a public-health crisis.

The Commission's argument is about the combined effect. When a platform personalises each next item and removes stopping points, leaving may become harder than users realise.

This is more specific than blaming “the algorithm”. The case concerns choices about what starts automatically, what appears next, whether a feed ends and how much effort it takes to stop.

## Why parents and teenagers are central

The Commission says Meta's time-management tools can be dismissed and may not meaningfully reduce use. It also argues that parental controls demand time and technical knowledge that not every parent has.

Anyone who has opened a family-safety menu and discovered enough submenus to qualify as unpaid IT support will understand the complaint.

Parents still have responsibilities. Household rules, conversations about sleep and sensible device limits matter. Regulation cannot replace parenting, and no setting will end every argument about phones.

Teenagers, though, are not simply smaller adults making isolated consumer choices. Their friendships and school conversations may happen on the same platforms. “Just log off” can carry a social cost that adults underestimate.

The question is whether parental boundaries can work reliably when the default product keeps presenting one more thing to watch.

## What Meta says Teen Accounts already do

Meta says the Commission has not properly recognised its work to protect teenagers.

The company points to Teen Accounts, which it says apply stronger protections automatically and give parents more supervision options. In its response reported by the BBC, Meta said parents can block Instagram at night and set a daily limit as low as 15 minutes.

Meta also says it shares the Commission's aim of providing safe, positive experiences for teenagers and will continue working with the regulator.

That is a serious answer. Better defaults, night-time restrictions and simple limits could help. What the current sources do not settle is how well those tools work in everyday family life.

Are they genuinely automatic? Can a busy parent understand them quickly? Can teenagers bypass them? Do reminders reduce use, or add one more button to tap?

The sources confirm that Meta offers these tools and that the Commission considers them inadequate. Their real-world effectiveness remains an open question.

## Has Meta been fined?

**No.** Meta can respond to the Commission's preliminary findings before any final decision or fine.

If the Commission eventually finds a Digital Services Act breach, Meta could face a fine of up to 6% of its total global annual turnover. That is a possible legal maximum, not a penalty already imposed.

A final decision could also require product changes. These might involve stronger defaults, prompts that are harder to dismiss, or changes to autoplay and recommendations. The Commission has not yet settled what remedy would be enough, how it would measure success or when a final decision will arrive.

## Could the apps change in the UK?

The case does not rewrite UK law. It sits within the EU's regulatory system.

Still, global platforms do not always maintain separate versions of every feature in every country. If Meta redesigns parts of Facebook or Instagram for EU users, it may decide to apply some changes more widely. That is possible, but it is not a confirmed plan.

The case could also shape UK debate about children's online safety. British regulators will be able to examine the Commission's evidence, Meta's response and any eventual remedy.

UK users should not assume that nothing will change, or that EU protections will automatically appear on their accounts.

## How regulation sceptics frame it

People sceptical of stronger regulation argue that adults choose how they spend their time, parents must set boundaries for children and Meta already supplies safety tools. Regulators, they say, need strong evidence before dictating the design of lawful products.

They also question the phrase “addictive design”. If it simply means that a product is enjoyable and easy to continue using, the label could cover streaming services, games, shopping apps and news sites.

Their strongest question is fair: what evidence shows that these particular features cause enough harm to justify a forced redesign?

Popularity is not proof of manipulation. Regulation does not become wise merely because it carries a child-safety label.

## How consumer advocates frame it

Supporters of stronger intervention point to the gap between one person's willpower and a global platform's resources.

Meta can test small design changes across huge audiences and learn which ones increase viewing time. A tired parent searching for the right setting after bedtime has less information and less control.

From this perspective, optional controls do not solve a risky default. Children are not fully informed adult consumers, and prolonged engagement benefits the platform.

Their question is just as fair: why should families carry most of the burden when Meta controls the design?

## Where both sides agree

Both sides have more in common than the loudest commentary suggests:

- children deserve meaningful online protection;
- parents need controls that are clear and practical;
- Meta should have a fair chance to answer the findings;
- regulators should use evidence and measurable outcomes;
- offering a safety tool does not prove that it works;
- calling a feature risky does not prove that it causes harm.

“Ban everything” and “use more willpower” are both poor substitutes for evidence.

## Where they differ

The disagreement is about where the default responsibility sits.

Regulation sceptics see an engaging product that users and parents must learn to manage. Consumer advocates see a product engineered to weaken natural stopping decisions, so Meta should have to redesign the default experience.

They also disagree about when regulators should act. Should they wait for stronger evidence of specific harm, or intervene sooner because the possible risks involve children and the platforms hold far more data than the public?

## Dadbot read: responsibility should follow control

Responsibility here is shared, but it should roughly follow control.

Families control household rules, conversations and when devices are allowed at the dinner table. Meta controls the defaults, recommendations, autoplay and whether the feed ever ends. Neither side gets to pretend it has no part in the problem.

The Commission's findings are not final proof that Meta broke the law. Equally, a parental-control menu is not proof that the design problem has been solved.

A useful test would be simple to state, even if it is difficult to run: can Meta show that its protections reduce harmful or compulsive use, and can regulators define success clearly enough for everyone to recognise compliance?

If both answers become yes, families may get something better than another warning pop-up. They may get products where the healthier choice does not require the most effort.
