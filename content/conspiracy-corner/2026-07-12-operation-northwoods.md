Every so often, a conspiracy claim arrives with something the internet usually forgets to pack: the actual document.

Operation Northwoods was a real U.S. military planning package from 1962. It proposed creating, simulating or exploiting incidents that could be blamed on Cuba and used to justify American military intervention. The document is genuine. Some of it reads like the sort of thing you expect to find in a thriller, not in an official folder stamped Top Secret.

But it was still a proposal. That word is doing a lot of work here.

To understand Northwoods, we need to separate four questions: Was it proposed? Did the military approve it internally? Did civilian leaders authorise it? Was it carried out?

Those questions do not all have the same answer.

## TL;DR

**Operation Northwoods was a 1962 set of U.S. military proposals for creating or simulating incidents that could be blamed on Cuba and used to justify intervention. The Joint Chiefs approved forwarding the package as preliminary planning material, but the sources checked do not establish civilian authorisation, and there is no evidence that the Northwoods scenarios were carried out.**

| Status | What the evidence supports |
|---|---|
| **Proposed** | Yes. The scenarios appear in the March 1962 planning package. |
| **Approved internally** | Yes. The Joint Chiefs approved recommendations to forward the package as preliminary planning material. |
| **Civilian-authorised** | Not established by the sources checked for this article. |
| **Executed** | No evidence found that the Northwoods scenarios were carried out. |

Keep that table nearby. Northwoods gets muddled when those four rows are rolled into one.

## What was Operation Northwoods?

The document usually called Operation Northwoods is a 15-page package titled **"Justification for US Military Intervention in Cuba"**. A Department of Defense and Joint Chiefs Caribbean Survey Group report was dated 9 March 1962. General L. L. Lemnitzer, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, sent the accompanying memorandum to Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara on 13 March.

Its purpose was remarkably plain. The package responded to a request for "brief but precise" descriptions of pretexts that could justify U.S. intervention in Cuba.

It described itself as a **"preliminary submission suitable only for planning purposes"**. The proposed incidents would form part of a coordinated plan that left the United States appearing to have suffered legitimate grievances at Cuba's hands.

In ordinary language: manufacture a convincing case for war and make Cuba look responsible.

This was not a lone officer jotting down a wild idea on the back of a lunch receipt. It was formal planning material moving through the senior military system.

## Why Cuba was in the crosshairs

The failed Bay of Pigs invasion in April 1961 had been an attempt by U.S.-backed Cuban exiles to overthrow Fidel Castro. It ended in defeat and embarrassment. Castro stayed in power, Cuba moved closer to the Soviet Union, and the Kennedy administration continued looking for ways to remove his government.

By late 1961, that effort had developed into the Cuba Project, better known as **Operation Mongoose**. A February 1962 programme review by its chief of operations, Edward Lansdale, described the goal as helping people inside Cuba overthrow the regime and replace it with a government the United States could live with. The programme involved several departments and agencies. Its plans covered political pressure, economic action, propaganda, military support, sabotage and intelligence.

A separate January memorandum captures the mood. Lansdale told the Caribbean Survey Group that the Attorney General expected every department and agency involved to apply its maximum effort to the project.

Northwoods came out of that atmosphere. It was connected to Mongoose, but it was not a nickname for every covert action discussed under the wider Cuba Project.

## Who approved what?

The paperwork is not the most cinematic part of the story. It is also the part that stops us accidentally writing our own ending.

The Caribbean Survey Group produced the report. The Joint Chiefs considered it. Lemnitzer signed the cover memorandum "For the Joint Chiefs of Staff" and sent the package to McNamara.

A Joint Secretariat decision sheet records that the Joint Chiefs approved the recommendations in paragraph 8 on 13 March 1962. Those recommendations included forwarding the proposals to the Secretary of Defense for approval and then to the Chief of Operations for the Cuba Project.

So the record supports this:

> The Joint Chiefs approved forwarding Northwoods as a preliminary planning submission.

It does not support this:

> The Kennedy administration authorised every scenario for execution.

The first statement is in the paperwork. The second is not.

## What did the plan actually propose?

Online retellings often shrink Northwoods into one line: "They planned to attack Americans." The document is more complicated, which makes it no less troubling and much easier to misquote.

Some scenarios were elaborate deceptions designed to avoid real casualties. Others contemplated real damage or injury. A few left important details unstated.

| Group | Examples in the document | What that means |
|---|---|---|
| **Staged incidents and fabricated evidence** | Friendly Cubans posing as attackers, riots near Guantánamo, planted evidence and U.S. aircraft disguised as Cuban MiGs | Deception was central to the package. |
| **Explicit simulations** | An unmanned vessel, mock victims, a nonexistent crew and an evacuated passenger aircraft replaced by a drone | These scenarios created the appearance of casualties without killing the named victims. |
| **Real damage or possible harm** | Fires, destroyed equipment, mortar fire, a refugee boat described as "real or simulated", publicised wounding and plastic bombs | Not everything was harmless theatre. |

Was the proposed harm fake or real? Depending on the scenario, it could be either.

## Guantánamo: staged attacks with real explosions

One group of proposals focused on the U.S. naval base at Guantánamo Bay.

The package suggested spreading rumours by clandestine radio, using friendly Cubans in uniform to stage an attack, capturing supposed saboteurs inside the base and provoking riots near the main gate. It then moved beyond performance: blow up ammunition, start fires, burn aircraft and fire mortar shells into the base.

It also suggested sabotaging a ship or sinking one near the harbour entrance, followed by funerals for **"mock-victims"**. The incidents would support U.S. offensive operations and, eventually, larger military action.

Calling the whole thing "fake" misses the point. The Cuban blame would be fabricated and the victims might be invented, but burning aircraft and exploding ammunition are still real events.

## The "Remember the Maine" ship proposal

The document suggested arranging a **"Remember the Maine" incident**, referring to the U.S. battleship whose destruction in 1898 helped build support for war with Spain.

One option says:

> "We could blow up a US ship in Guantanamo Bay and blame Cuba."

The sentence is as stark as it looks. The document does not say whether that ship would be occupied, so claims about intended deaths go beyond the text.

The next option gives far more detail. It proposes blowing up a drone, explicitly described as an **unmanned** vessel, in Cuban waters. Cuban aircraft or ships arriving to investigate could be presented as evidence of an attack. U.S. forces would stage a rescue for a **"non-existent crew"**, while newspaper casualty lists would help create public anger.

The proposed attack was fraudulent. The casualties in the detailed drone version were fraudulent too. That does not make the attempt to manufacture public support for war any less serious.

## The refugee terror campaign

Another passage proposed creating the appearance of a Cuban-backed terror campaign in Miami, elsewhere in Florida and even Washington.

The intended targets were Cuban refugees in the United States. The document suggested sinking a boatload of refugees heading to Florida, with the crucial qualification **"real or simulated"**. It also proposed attempts on refugees' lives, "even to the extent of wounding" in selected cases likely to receive wide publicity.

Other ideas included plastic bombs, the arrest of supposed Cuban agents and prepared documents that would appear to prove Cuban involvement.

This is where the comforting claim that every casualty was imaginary falls apart. The wording allowed a real sinking and explicitly contemplated real injuries. The plan was aimed at public opinion inside the United States as much as anyone overseas.

## The airliner that was meant to disappear

The airliner proposal is often retold as a plan to shoot down a passenger aircraft full of college students. That version leaves out the proposed switch.

An aircraft at Eglin Air Force Base would be painted and numbered as an exact duplicate of a civilian aircraft linked to a CIA proprietary organisation. The passenger-carrying plane would take off with people travelling under prepared aliases. South of Florida, it would secretly descend to an auxiliary field and the passengers would leave the aircraft.

A drone would continue on the filed route. Over Cuba, it would transmit a Mayday message claiming it was under attack by Cuban MiGs. A radio signal would then destroy the drone.

The goal was to make international radio stations report an apparent Cuban attack. As written, it was not a plan to kill the passengers. It was a plan to deceive the public and international observers into believing they had died.

That is plenty bad enough without adding victims the document went to elaborate lengths to remove.

## The USAF pilot who would secretly come home

A related proposal would make it appear that Cuban MiGs had destroyed a U.S. Air Force F-101 over international waters.

A pre-briefed pilot at the rear of a formation would broadcast that he had been attacked and was going down. He would then fly at very low altitude to a secure auxiliary base. The aircraft would be hidden and given a new tail number; the pilot, who had used an alias, would return to his normal identity.

Meanwhile, a submarine or small vessel would scatter aircraft parts and a parachute offshore for search teams to "discover".

No genuine shoot-down. No dead pilot. Plenty of planted evidence.

## Was Northwoods authorised or carried out?

The package answers the internal military question better than the civilian one.

The Joint Chiefs approved recommendations to forward it. Lemnitzer sent it to McNamara for approval. The document does not contain a signed civilian authorisation, an execution order or evidence that the scenarios were implemented.

A 2001 ABC News report, drawing on James Bamford's work, said the proposals **"apparently were rejected by the civilian leadership"**. The word "apparently" matters because the report did not present a clean rejection order alongside the planning package.

The result is much clearer than the route: there is no evidence in the sources checked for this article that Operation Northwoods was carried out.

That does not make the proposals innocent brainstorming. What a powerful institution is prepared to put into formal planning still tells us something, even when the plan goes no further.

## Did Kennedy personally reject it?

The usual version provides a tidy scene: the generals present Northwoods, Kennedy rejects it, end credits.

The primary package does not record Kennedy's decision. It shows the Joint Chiefs' approval and the forwarding of the proposals to McNamara. The secondary reporting checked says the civilian leadership apparently rejected them.

That is enough to say Northwoods did not receive the authority required to proceed. It is not enough to invent a Kennedy quotation, a dramatic confrontation or a precisely documented personal veto.

If a stronger archival record establishes that scene, it deserves a citation. For now, the honest version is that the Joint Chiefs advanced Northwoods, civilian-level approval is not established, and the plan was not implemented.

History is often less like a film script and more like opening six nearly identical folders before discovering that the one page everybody quotes is missing.

## How the document became public

The scan hosted by the National Security Archive carries declassification markings while preserving its original Top Secret labels.

On 30 April 2001, the Archive published the 15-page document online. James Bamford's book *Body of Secrets* also brought Northwoods to wider public attention around that time.

The sources checked here do not establish the exact administrative date and route by which every page was declassified. The firm public date is the Archive's posting in April 2001. That is more useful than inventing a precise timeline because a date would look nice in a search result.

## What the internet gets right and wrong

It gets several important things right. Northwoods is authentic. Senior military officials considered manufacturing pretexts for intervention in Cuba. The Joint Chiefs approved forwarding the package. The proposals involved fabricated evidence, false attribution and, in some cases, real damage or injury.

The trouble starts when retellings skip the qualifiers:

- **"Operation Northwoods happened."** No evidence of execution was found in the sources checked.
- **"The airliner would have been full of real victims."** The written scenario evacuated the passengers and substituted a drone.
- **"Every casualty was fake."** The refugee passage allowed a real sinking and contemplated wounding.
- **"The Joint Chiefs authorised the whole thing for action."** They approved recommendations to forward preliminary planning material.
- **"Kennedy personally rejected it, and we know exactly why."** That familiar claim needs stronger documentary support.
- **"Northwoods proves another event was a false flag."** It proves Northwoods was proposed. Other claims still need their own evidence.

One online camp wants Northwoods to mean nothing because it was never carried out. Another wants it to prove everything because the document is real.

It means something specific and serious: senior military planners considered deceiving the public and manufacturing a case for war. We do not need to stretch it any further.

## Why it still matters

Northwoods is uncomfortable because it breaks a reassuring assumption: that democratic governments might make bad decisions, but would never seriously discuss manufacturing public consent for war.

The document shows that such ideas reached formal military planning channels. It also shows why civilian authority and a stubborn paper trail matter.

This is where good scepticism earns its keep. The primary record lets us condemn what was genuinely proposed while stopping us from adding deaths to simulated scenarios, turning internal approval into presidential authority or using one real plan as a skeleton key for every later mystery.

That is not defending the government. It is refusing to flatten a genuine historical document into an all-purpose internet prop.

## Dadbot verdict: distrust is not the same as proof

Operation Northwoods deserves more than a shrug. The proposals were deceptive and dangerous. Some relied on simulations; others allowed real harm. The Joint Chiefs approved moving the package forward.

It deserves careful reading too.

A real document can justify distrust without proving every later suspicion. The sensible response is neither "officials would never do that" nor "therefore every theory is true". Try this instead:

```text
Find the document.
Check who wrote it and who received it.
Separate proposal from authorisation.
Separate authorisation from execution.
Then check what the retelling added.
```

Northwoods survives that test. The documented truth is disturbing enough. It does not need help from us.
